# Find causal claims

A causal claim says that one thing produces, changes, enables, prevents, or
otherwise influences another. Loom records the generalized claim made by the
article, not every observation reported in it.

## Look for a directed relationship

Ask three questions:

1. What is presented as the cause?
2. What changes or occurs as the effect?
3. Does the article present the relationship as causal?

Common causal wording includes *causes*, *drives*, *increases*, *reduces*,
*promotes*, *inhibits*, *leads to*, and *results in*. These words are clues,
not proof. Read enough surrounding text to understand what the authors claim.

Do not turn a statement of association into a causal claim. For example,
“canopy cover was associated with seedling abundance” does not by itself say
that canopy cover caused the difference. Record the claim only if the article
provides causal language or context that supports that interpretation.

## Decide where one claim ends

Create a separate edge for each distinct cause-to-effect relationship.

For example:

> Nitrogen addition increased grass biomass, which reduced light at the soil
> surface and suppressed seedling establishment.

This passage may support several relationships:

- nitrogen addition → grass biomass;
- grass biomass → soil-surface light; and
- soil-surface light → seedling establishment.

Do not combine a chain into one edge if doing so hides the intermediate causal
steps stated by the article.

## Preserve enough context

A claim may span more than one sentence. Add each necessary passage to the
excerpt bin, then select all passages that directly support the node or edge.

Include enough text to make the interpretation reviewable, but avoid selecting
entire sections when a sentence or short passage is sufficient.

## What not to record as a causal edge

Do not create causal edges for:

- bibliographic facts;
- isolated measurements or species-occurrence records;
- purely descriptive statements;
- methods that do not express a causal relationship;
- relationships you infer but the article does not claim; or
- background knowledge added from memory.

Claims can appear in the abstract, introduction, results, discussion, figure
captions, or other article sections. Their location does not determine whether
they are causal.

## A working example

The remaining guide uses this simplified claim:

> Experimental nitrogen addition increased above-ground plant biomass.

It contains:

- a cause: nitrogen addition;
- an effect: plant biomass increases; and
- evidence wording: the addition was experimental.

The exact node and edge fields will depend on the active schema. The example is
about decomposing the claim, not prescribing ontology terms.
